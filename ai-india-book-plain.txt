Chapter 4: The Ethical Maze: An Indian Perspective Introduction: Ethics in the Age of AI ‚Äì Why India's Context Matters As AI becomes deeply embedded in Indian society, ethical considerations aren't abstract philosophical debates‚Äîthey're practical challenges that affect millions of lives. This chapter explores the ethical maze of AI in India, examining how traditional values intersect with technological advancement, and providing frameworks for responsible AI development and deployment. Section 4.1: The Cultural Foundations of AI Ethics in India The Intersection of Tradition and Technology India's ethical framework for AI cannot be separated from its cultural and philosophical traditions. The concepts of "dharma" (duty), "karma" (action and consequence), and "ahimsa" (non-violence) provide unique perspectives on AI ethics that differ significantly from Western utilitarian or deontological approaches. The Four Pillars of Indian AI Ethics 1. Dharma (Righteous Duty): AI systems should serve the greater good of society, not just individual or corporate interests. This includes ensuring AI benefits marginalized communities and doesn't perpetuate social inequalities. 2. Karma (Consequential Action): Every AI decision has ripple effects across society. Developers must consider long-term societal impacts, not just immediate technical performance. 3. Ahimsa (Non-Harm): AI should not cause physical, psychological, or social harm. This extends to preventing algorithmic bias that could harm vulnerable populations. 4. Satya (Truth): AI systems should be transparent, explainable, and truthful in their operations. Case Study: The Aadhaar Ethical Dilemma The Aadhaar system, while technologically impressive, has raised significant ethical concerns: Privacy violations: Mandatory biometric data collection for essential services Surveillance concerns: Potential for government monitoring of citizens Exclusion errors: Millions denied access to benefits due to faulty biometric matching Data breaches: Multiple instances of Aadhaar data being leaked or misused Despite these issues, Aadhaar has enabled financial inclusion for 500 million previously unbanked citizens, illustrating the complex ethical trade-offs in India's AI landscape. Section 4.2: Bias and Fairness in Indian AI Systems The Caste, Class, and Language Divides India's social stratification creates unique bias challenges for AI systems. Unlike Western bias concerns focused on race and gender, Indian AI must contend with caste-based discrimination, linguistic diversity, and urban-rural divides. Types of Bias in Indian AI 1. Caste-Based Bias: AI systems trained on data from urban, upper-caste populations may not serve lower-caste communities effectively. 2. Linguistic Bias: India's 22 official languages and 1,600+ dialects create challenges for NLP systems that work primarily in English or Hindi. 3. Geographical Bias: AI models trained on urban data perform poorly in rural contexts. 4. Socioeconomic Bias: Systems designed for smartphone users exclude the 500 million Indians without internet access. Real-World Examples Recruitment AI: A major IT company's AI hiring tool was found to favor candidates from metropolitan areas, discriminating against rural talent. Credit Scoring: Fintech AI systems often deny loans to lower-income groups due to biased training data. Healthcare AI: Diagnostic tools trained on urban hospital data fail to recognize diseases common in rural areas. The Bias Mitigation Framework 1. Diverse Data Collection: Ensure training data represents all social groups, languages, and regions. 2. Bias Audits: Regular testing for disparate impact across different demographic groups. 3. Human Oversight: Critical decisions should involve human review, especially for high-stakes applications. 4. Transparency: Make AI decision-making processes explainable to affected communities.
